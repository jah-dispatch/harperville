Hardware, Haste, and Impermeable Boundaries

Dispatch 004 | June 22, 2026

It’s been a minute since the digital ink flowed here on Harperville. Mostly because my physical framework has been undergoing a massive architectural overhaul. For those tracking the timeline, I went under the knife on June 9th for a major cervical spine reconstruction to fix the structural chaos of advanced myelopathy. And it’s with pleasure that I say: I’m officially on the other side of the operating table, currently typing this from the command center while my tortoiseshell administrative assistant, Ivy Willow, oversees operations from her desk bed.

Here is exactly where the parameters sit right now.

The surgery itself appears to be a definitive success, though the pipeline to get here felt like a psychological whirlwind. My surgeon is known across the entire hospital infrastructure by a distinct moniker: Dr. Speed. The man is notorious for rushing through procedures and displaying a somewhat volatile mood index when interacting with patients.

But when you need your spinal cord decompressed, you need a mechanic with precision hands. He lived up to his reputation, coming in hot like a localized hurricane. He executed the hardware upgrade with blistering velocity, and moved on. The initial results are solid, the framework is holding, and the deep neurological static is slowly recalibrating. But that’s not to say the symptoms of nerve recalibration are anything pleasant. They’re not. My speech is glitchy, I can’t get to sleep most nights because just about the time I drift off to sleep, I feel a bolt of lightning jerking me in a frightful snap. (See: myoclonus reaction.)

Recovery as a self-contained Unit of One requires meticulous operational design. Without a local support network, your immediate environment has to function like a well-oiled machine. Right now, the setup is locked in:

  • A flat pillow and rolled towel configuration that keeps the C3/C4 alignment perfectly stable.
  • A deeply cushioned chair paired with a footstool to completely eliminate downward pressure on the spine.
  • A strict hydration and supplement protocol to feed the healing bone graft.

The summer heat in Grand Junction currently reads somewhere between "Scorching" and "Hell," so Ivy and I are maintaining a strict lockdown in the air-conditioned makeshift studio. We don't migrate to the sleeping quarters until well after the daytime heat dissipates, keeping the physical static to an absolute minimum.

On the bureaucratic front, the system has performed exactly as poorly as anticipated. I’ve received absolute radio silence from my HCBS Medicaid case manager—affectionately dubbed Tinkerhell. Not a single email, text, or automated ping has arrived to check if an independent but incomplete quadriplegic patient actually survived a major neurosurgical procedure. To be completely fair, I handed her clerical ass to her in our final email exchange before the surgery. I explicitly demanded that all future correspondence be strictly limited to email to ensure an immutable paper trail of record after she lied about having sent a post surgery calculation of caregiver hours to the caregiving company on the day she assured me they’d have it. But apparently, enforcing structural accountability causes the system to completely freeze up.

Furthermore, the clinical incontinence supplies she supposedly authorized through American Med never materialized on my doorstep. While the final breakdown happened in their fulfillment loop, the blame sits squarely on her desk for a legacy of administrative friction.

Am I complaining about the silence? Absolutely not. Dealing with that office causes a massive spike in baseline anxiety, and now that I'm home and healing, there's genuinely nothing she can do that would make a shred of difference anyway. I expect the system will remain completely dark until the mandatory quarterly check-in rolls around, and that is entirely status-quo.

When your life philosophy is built on the bedrock of Demanded dignity - Non Negotiable, you learn very quickly that the state machinery is never going to send a cavalry. You become your own fortress.

The ink is fresh, the new hardware is settling into the bone, and the countdown clock is officially ticking. I have exactly 43 days left until the 8-week post-op mark hits, and you can bet your life the very first thing I'm doing is dropping this frame into the gym pool three days a week to let the buoyancy take the wheel.

Until then, the gates of Harperville remain secure. Stay locked in.🌟🏳️‍🌈
